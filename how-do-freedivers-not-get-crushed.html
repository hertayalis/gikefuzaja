How do freedivers not get crushed?

 Posted on June 4, 2024  |  7 minutes  |  1279 words  |  Reinaldo Massengill
Scuba divers and free divers rely on equalising to prevent damaging their bodies. They compensate for static water pressure by adding equal gas pressure into their air spaces as the atmospheric pressure of the depth they are diving in. This prevents their air spaces from collapsing under pressure.

How do freedivers not get the bends?

Decompression sickness (DCS) after freediving is very rare. Freedivers simply do not on-gas enough nitrogen to provoke DCS. Thus, very few cases of DCS in freedivers have ever been reported, and these have involved repeated deep dives in a short time frame.

How do freedivers survive the pressure?

Freedivers use specialized masks with a low internal volume, as the amount of air they have available to equalize the space in the mask is limited by the air they take down with them. The smaller the volume, the deeper and longer you can dive, as less of your precious air is will be required to equalize your mask.

How do freedivers not get affected by pressure?

Freedivers do not inhale pressurized air, but the final breath we take before a dive still contains nitrogen from the atmosphere, which will still pressurize at depth. Nitrogen accumulation still happens, just very little compared to scuba divers.

How do freedivers hold their breath?

Freedivers go underwater without the help of scuba gear or a breathing apparatus. Freedivers simply hold their breath for as long as they can before returning to the surface. Thus, a freediver can only travel as long under the surface as their lungs will take them.

What happens to a free diver's body when they ascend?

If a diver ascends too quickly, the nitrogen gas in his body will expand at such a rate that he is unable to eliminate it efficiently, and the nitrogen will form small bubbles in his tissues. This is known as decompression sickness, and can be very painful, lead to tissue death, and even be life threatening.
